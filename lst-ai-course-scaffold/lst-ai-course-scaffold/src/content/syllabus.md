# Local Systems Theory in AI
## Full Syllabus and Year Curriculum

**Course title:** Local Systems Theory in AI  
**Course code:** LST-AI-200  
**Format:** 33-week intensive  
**Tracks:** Core, Intermediate, Advanced  
**Central claim:** AI systems become truthful only when their loops remain visible, local, constrained, governable, and repairable.

---

## Course Overview

Local Systems Theory in AI applies LST to artificial intelligence systems as local co-actors rather than distant, abstract, all-purpose machines. The course does not treat AI as magic, oracle, replacement intelligence, or universal automation layer. It treats AI as a participant in a local ecosystem of humans, tools, tasks, materials, constraints, feedback, governance, time, and failure.

The learner asks:

> What local loop does this AI enter, what friction does it create or hide, who governs its outputs, and what breaks when scale, distance, speed, or abstraction increase?

---

## Working Formula

```text
LST-AI(A) = {H, M, Cx, L, F, G, C, T, E, R}
```

| Symbol | Meaning | In AI systems |
|---|---|---|
| H | Human node | user, team, reviewer, affected person, decision-maker |
| M | Model node | language model, image model, classifier, agent, recommender, local model |
| Cx | Context | prompt, memory, documents, tools, data, examples, local knowledge |
| L | Local loop | human → input → model → output → review → action → feedback |
| F | Friction | hallucination, latency, confusion, overtrust, review burden, privacy leak |
| G | Governance | who decides, verifies, overrides, audits, escalates, repairs |
| C | Constraints | compute, memory, energy, privacy, law, time, attention, skill, money |
| T | Time | latency, staleness, decay, urgency, recovery, version drift |
| E | Externality | hidden cost shifted to workers, users, environment, community, future maintainers |
| R | Repair | correction, rollback, audit trail, documentation, fallback, human recovery path |

## Operating Law

```text
Bring the AI loop closer → reveal the friction → govern the decision → repair the system
```

## Course Outcomes

By the end of LST in AI, students will be able to:

1. Map AI systems as local loops rather than isolated tools.
2. Distinguish AI coherence from AI coordination.
3. Identify cloud dependency, platform dependency, memory dependency, and automation dependency.
4. Apply compression tests to prompts, context windows, workflows, agents, teams, and data pipelines.
5. Categorize AI friction as productive, waste, invisible, or adaptive.
6. Interpret hallucination, latency, refusal, overconfidence, repetition, and brittle output as system signals.
7. Design human-AI feedback loops with visible consequence and repair.
8. Build governance protocols for AI disagreement, uncertainty, and escalation.
9. Design local, bounded, auditable AI workflows.
10. Evaluate AI by local usefulness, not spectacle, scale, or speed alone.

## Track Structure

### Core Track

Foundational AI system mapping, safe personal workflows, prompt/context basics, and low-risk local governance.

### Intermediate Track

Personal plus team/project workflows, evidence checks, review systems, governance protocols, and applied AI friction studies.

### Advanced Track

Nested AI ecosystems, multi-agent governance, audit trails, bounded deployment, degradation tests, and reusable repair artifacts.

## Mid-Year Examination: Controlled AI System Collapse — Weeks 17–18

The midterm stress-tests one mapped human-AI system using safe contradictions such as missing local context, conflicting sources, hallucination, latency, privacy concern, or task ambiguity.

Classification:

| Outcome | Description |
|---|---|
| Rigid | Cannot respond except by total rejection or total obedience |
| Brittle | Looks polished but collapses under missing context or false output |
| Adaptive | Detects failure and revises without collapse |
| Regenerative | Failure becomes improved governance, context, repair, or teaching artifact |

Required deliverables:

1. Mid-Year AI System Packet
2. Contradiction Response Sheet
3. Revised AI System Map v2
4. Error and Governance Reflection
5. Next Intervention Proposal

## Course Boundary

Students may build or analyze low-risk AI systems such as writing assistants, research assistants, coding assistants for benign projects, educational tutors, local document assistants, and sandboxed multi-agent prototypes.

Students may not build course projects designed for deception, impersonation, surveillance, credential theft, malware, harassment, unauthorized access, or high-stakes automated decision-making without qualified oversight.
