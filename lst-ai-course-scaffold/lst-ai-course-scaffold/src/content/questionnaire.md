# LST in AI — 20-Question Placement Assessment

| Score | Track |
|---:|---|

The enrollment applicant form appears only after all 20 questions are answered.

1. When you think about AI, you usually see it as:  
A. A tool that helps people do tasks faster.  
B. A powerful assistant that needs careful prompting.  
C. A system component that changes workflows and decisions.  
D. A co-actor inside a local human-machine ecosystem.

2. When an AI gives a wrong answer, your first instinct is to:  
A. Try a better prompt.  
B. Ask the model again or use a different model.  
C. Ask what context, evidence, or review process was missing.  
D. Treat the error as system data revealing a loop, dependency, or governance failure.

3. What does an AI hallucination usually mean to you?  
A. The model made something up.  
B. The prompt or model was not good enough.  
C. The system lacked grounding or verification.  
D. The local loop allowed plausible output to outrun evidence.

4. What is the best way to make an AI workflow trustworthy?  
A. Use a better model.  
B. Write better prompts.  
C. Add evidence, review, and correction loops.  
D. Keep output close to local context, governed action, and visible repair.

5. What does AI friction usually mean?  
A. Something annoying that should be removed.  
B. A usability problem.  
C. A signal that the workflow needs diagnosis.  
D. Structural truth appearing at the boundary between model, human, task, and context.

6. If an AI workflow works only with unlimited cloud access, unlimited context, and perfect connectivity, it is:  
A. Normal modern AI.  
B. Useful but dependent.  
C. Untested under local constraint.  
D. Coordinated around abundance rather than coherent.

7. What is prompt engineering closest to?  
A. Asking clearly.  
B. Learning model tricks.  
C. Designing a compressed task boundary.  
D. Constructing a local governance and context interface.

8. What should happen before AI output is acted on?  
A. It should sound right.  
B. It should be reviewed if important.  
C. It should pass evidence and risk checks.  
D. It should enter a visible loop of review, accountability, escalation, and repair.

9. What is the main danger of frictionless AI?  
A. People may become lazy.  
B. It may make too many things easy.  
C. It may hide error, cost, privacy loss, and judgment decay.  
D. It may shift invisible burden to affected people, workers, environment, or future maintainers.

10. What is the smallest meaningful AI system?  
A. A prompt and response.  
B. A useful tool.  
C. A human-AI feedback loop.  
D. A governed local loop from need to output to action to consequence to repair.

11. What makes local AI valuable?  
A. It is private.  
B. It is fast and convenient.  
C. It keeps context and feedback close.  
D. It preserves proximity, constraint, accountability, and repair.

12. What should happen when two AI agents disagree?  
A. Pick the answer that sounds best.  
B. Ask a stronger model to decide.  
C. Compare evidence and route uncertainty.  
D. Use a visible governance protocol with authority, escalation, and rollback.

13. How should AI memory be treated?  
A. More memory is usually better.  
B. Memory is useful but can get messy.  
C. Memory should be curated, refreshed, and limited.  
D. Memory is a local system with decay, consent, priority, and repair requirements.

14. What is automation debt?  
A. The cost of buying AI tools.  
B. The time spent fixing automations.  
C. Hidden future work created by premature automation.  
D. The accumulated burden of opaque decisions, skill loss, repair delay, and trust damage.

15. What makes an AI system ethical?  
A. It helps people.  
B. It follows rules.  
C. It protects privacy and avoids obvious harm.  
D. It preserves agency, consent, accountability, transparency, repair, and exit.

16. What should be measured in an AI workflow?  
A. Speed and output quality.  
B. Time saved and user satisfaction.  
C. Accuracy, review burden, error type, and context quality.  
D. Local truth, hidden cost, governance failure, recovery time, and degradation under scale.

17. What happens when AI is scaled too quickly?  
A. More users benefit.  
B. More errors may appear.  
C. Context and governance become harder.

18. What should an AI final project prove?  
A. I know how to use AI tools.  
B. I can build a useful AI workflow.  
C. I can map, test, and repair a human-AI system.  
D. My AI system can break, reveal truth, regenerate, and remain locally accountable.

19. What is your preferred relationship to AI?  
A. Useful assistant.  
B. Creative partner.  
C. Governed collaborator.  
D. Bounded co-actor inside a visible local system.

20. Why do you want to study LST in AI?  
A. To use AI better.  
B. To improve my workflows.
